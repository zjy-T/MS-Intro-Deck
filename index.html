Era One · 2018–2021

AI for Science

MACHINE LEARNING AS A RESEARCH TOOL

Graduate thesis · Tsinghua University

Electrochemical CO₂ reduction (CO₂RR) converts atmospheric CO₂ into fuel products — but product quality depends entirely on the catalyst.

High-Value Products
Ethylene, Ethanol — energy-dense compounds with real commercial demand
Low-Value Byproducts
Carbon Monoxide, Formate — common waste gases with limited industrial use
The Catalyst is the Key
A catalyst is a material that guides a chemical reaction toward specific products — like a filter that decides what comes out. Here, a copper-based catalyst steers CO₂ toward the valuable side.
The Goal Find which catalyst materials maximize high-value fuel production from CO₂.
  • Catalyst R&D typically involves computational simulations called "DFT calculations" before experimentation in a physical lab
  • Each simulation can take up to 2 days to complete on a supercomputer and cost up to $1200 in computational resources
  • A full screening of all material combinations will take years and is very infeasible
  • Need a cheap, high-throughput method for initial catalyst screening

Eight algorithms trained on the same data. Only tree-based models capture the nonlinear patterns.

Key insight: XGBR, GBR, and RFR achieve R² > 0.96 — their predictions closely match DFT ground truth. Simpler models (KRR, SVR) and the neural network fail on this small, nonlinear dataset.

Finding the signal in the noise: More features adds noise and overfitting. Too few loses predictive power. The optimum sits in between.

Mapping the chemical space: The model identifies which physical properties matter most for accurate prediction.

Usage-based billing cost $184 in just 4 days. Agentic loops burn tokens at a rate that's hard to predict upfront.
Subscriptions cap your flow
Usage limits break momentum. Waiting 3+ hours mid-build kills productivity — so you buy extra credits just to keep going.
Trial-and-error is the norm
Most of that spend isn't building — it's iterating, reverting, and retrying. The agent gets it wrong often, and each attempt costs real money.

AI has evolved from narrow, task-specific models to general-purpose agents that can build alongside you — unlocking workflows that simply didn't exist a few years ago.

What this means for Product Managers

Continuous learning is non-negotiable
The landscape shifts fast. Staying current is how you bring new experiences to customers — not just optimize existing ones.
The bottleneck is now judgment, not execution
Agents can build fast. Knowing what to build, when to push back, and what "good" looks like — that's the real skill.
Prototyping is becoming a PM superpower
Working demos come together fast. Validating ideas early and communicating vision concretely changes how you de-risk bets.
Building got cheaper — quality didn't
Getting started is fast, but polish demands time and iteration. Shipping quick is easy; shipping well still requires craft.
